04 - Trust Architecture

Where Trust Breaks Down

Critical

The Email Trust Architecture framework maps every email against a seven-stage trust progression: Unaware, Interested, Engaged, Buyer, Repeat Buyer, Advocate, Influencer. The core rule is the Rule of Adjacency: an email may only ask the reader to move one stage forward Any CTA that skips stages is a trust violation, and trust violations are why clicks don't convert to purchases.

Dermoi's data shows two overlapping diagnostic patterns from the trust framework:

✕
Pattern 1: High opens, low clicks. Subject lines create curiosity, but the email body does not create alignment. The reader opens, senses a mismatch between what the subject promised and what the email delivers, and leaves. This signals a gap between what your audience is interested in and what your emails are actually offering them.
✕
Pattern 2: Clicks present, purchases absent. The clearest trust friction signal. People are curious enough to click but not confident enough to buy. The Engaged-to-Buyer transition requires certainty, not just curiosity. Common causes: offer clarity gap, objections not resolved before the ask, perceived risk too high, identity mismatch.

These two patterns point to the same root cause: urgency is being used before certainty is built The framework diagnosis is that all four core trust problems are present in Dermoi's program:

The trust ratio across the campaign program reflects this imbalance. Roughly 35% of sends are trust deposits (educational content, product stories, ingredient education) and 65% are trust withdrawals (discount announcements, urgency-driven promotions, sale pushes). A healthy program runs the opposite ratio, or close to it.

The Core Diagnostic Finding

Dermoi's email program is failing because the trust cost of the asks exceeds the trust that has been earned at the point of asking. Rebuilding sequence logic so every email earns the right to make its ask before making it is the actual fix. That requires a systems rebuild, not a subject line rewrite.

✕

Structurally broken. £0.03 RPR despite 75%+ open rates. A subscriber who opened one email is at trust Stage 2 (Interested). This flow sends purchase CTAs to that subscriber, which is a two-stage trust leap. Opening an email is not a purchase signal.

!

The Structural Pattern

The two flows that work (Abandoned Cart and Checkout) share one thing: the trust stage of the subscriber matches the ask in the email Abandoned cart subscribers have already demonstrated purchase intent, so a purchase CTA is adjacent. Every other flow has a mismatch between where the subscriber is and what the email is asking for. That mismatch, multiplied across 30+ flows and millions of sends, is the program's core revenue leak.

Subject Lines
96% emoji usage. 34% urgency or discount language. No dual-hook structure.

Subject lines run at 96% emoji usage across all 91 campaigns. The intent — inbox differentiation — is reasonable, but 96% emoji rate means it provides no differentiation. It is the baseline. Second: 34% of subjects lead with urgency or discount, which conditions the list to treat non-urgent emails as low priority and discount emails as the only reason to act.

Subject line architecture that consistently drives clicks pairs a short, specific hook with preview text that completes the curiosity gap — not adds generic urgency to it. That structure is absent from all 91 campaigns. Every subject leads with emoji decoration, urgency pressure, or a generic benefit claim. None show the specificity pattern that drives consistent click-through across trust stages.

Body Copy
63.4% opens collapsing to 0.93% clicks is a body alignment failure.

No direct body copy access was provided, but the click pattern tells the structural story. A 63.4% open rate collapsing to 0.93% clicks means the subject line created an expectation the email body did not fulfil. The reader opened, scanned, found no clear next step calibrated to their trust level, and left. This pattern is consistent across all 91 campaigns. The fix is structural, not cosmetic — it requires realigning what the body delivers against what the subject line promised, at the trust level of the reader receiving it.

Calls to Action
Single CTA. Buried at the bottom. Zero trust ladder architecture.

Every campaign in the analyzed set uses a single CTA. None show a multi-CTA trust ladder. The result is binary: a subscriber either scrolls to the bottom and acts, or they don't. Readers ready to buy at word three never get an early action path. Readers almost ready who need one more proof point never get a mid-email nudge. A single buried CTA only captures the minority who read to the end.

✕

11 - What This Requires

What This Requires

The diagnosis is clear. The fix is not a subject line rewrite or a template refresh. What Dermoi's program needs is a trust architecture rebuild, which means rethinking the logic of every major flow, restructuring the campaign strategy from discount-dependent to value-led, and rebuilding the CTA architecture across the entire program.

This is systems work, not copy work. It requires understanding where each subscriber sits in the trust progression, what emotional state they are in at each trigger point, and what the correct ask is at that moment, not the most profitable ask in isolation.
